DISSECTED RECIPE


In order to understand the chemical processes involved in making bread, let's dissect a recipe from Taste of Home for Basic Homemade Bread.

  1. MIXING:

    In a large bowl, dissolve yeast in warm water. Add the sugar, salt, and 3 cups flour. Combine until smooth. Stir in enough remaining flour, 1/2 cup at a time, to form a soft dough.

    2. What's going on?


    When flour is mixed with water, the proteins in the flour, gliadin and glutenin, are hydrated and form gluten, which is a substance that gives elasticity to dough and helps it rise.

    celiac disease

    Celiac disease is “a condition caused by an abnormal sensitivity to gluten in the diet.” (Hill, Attack of the Gluten) The immune system of people with this disease mistakenly identifies parts of gliadin and glutenin—the two proteins in gluten—as “foreign” and sends out lymphocytes to destroy these foreign epitopes. When partially digested gluten enters the bloodstream from the intenstine, a series of reactions triggered by the epitopes results in the release of cykotines, leading to inflammation of the intestinal lining. This causes symptoms such as abdominal pain, diarrhea, skin rashes, and, ultimately, severe intestinal damage. However, there are many possible wheat substitutes in existence today, such as sorghum, hydroxymethylcellulose, and xantham gum.

    The role of the salt is to create a stronger and more stable dough as its ions (Na2+ and Cl-) shield the charges of gluten and allow the molecules to get closer.

    In this recipe, the leavening agent used is yeast, so the water helps to re-activate the dried yeast fungi.

  2. RISING:

    Cover and let rise in a warm place until doubled, about 1-1/2 hours.

    3. What's going on?


    The process of bread rising (also known as proving) involves the creation of bubbles of CO2 gas. The chemical reaction that produces carbon dioxide depends on the leavening agent used; for yeast, this process is fermentation, and for baking soda and baking powder, what occurs is an acid-base reaction. Each of the leavening agents used can be understood in more detail below.

    yeast

    Yeast are tiny single-celled organisms that feed on sugar in a process called fermentation to produce ethanol and carbon dioxide. The fermentation process can be summarized with this chemical equation: C6H12O6 ➝ 2CH3CH2OH + 2CO2 The carbon dioxide causes the dough to increase in volume and rise; the ethanol evaporates when the bread is baked. It also contributes to the "enticing aroma" of bread.


    yeast + honey

    When a combination of yeast and honey is used in baked goods, there tends to be a higher rise due to the additional glucose. As the yeast feeds on glucose during fermentation, the addition of honey means more CO2 will be produced.

    sourdough starter

    Instead of instant yeast, where yeast are dried and fored into a powder, with sourdough bread, sourdough yeast fungi live in a liquid medium known as a starter. Often, bakers will share starters, but many people also utilize the wild yeast in the air to create their own starters from scratch. (This is actually how the earliest bread was made -- sometimes accidentally!)The acidic flavor of sourdough bread comes from lactic acid, as the starter also contains a bacteria, lactobacilli. Along with the alcohol created by the yeast, this contributes to the unique flavor of this variety of bread.

    baking soda

    This is an acid-base reaction. Baking soda reacts with an acid (in the case of this experiment I used lemon juice, also known as citric acid) to create carbon dioxide and water: 3NaHCO3+C6H8O7 ⟶ 3Na++C6H5O73-+3H2O+3CO2.

    baking powder

    Baking powder produces CO2 in a way similar to baking soda, only it does not require the addition of a separate acid as it already contains a dry acid. It is composed of baking soda (sodium bicarbonate, NaHCO3) and a dry acid, usually cream of tartar (KHC4H4O6. After the addition of a water-based ingredient, these substances react to form CO2 (g) and other products: NaHCO3 + KHC4H4O6 → KNaC4H4O6 + H2O + CO2. There are two common types of baking powder that you can find on the shelf: single-acting and double-acting. Single-acting powder reacts to form carbon dioxide when the dough is mixed, whereas double-acting powder also makes CO2 when the mixture is heated.)

  3. KNEADING:

    Turn onto a floured surface; knead until smooth and elastic, 8-10 minutes. Place in a greased bowl, turning once to grease the top.

    4. What's going on?


    When bread dough is kneaded, the proteins are stretched out and aligned correctly with one another, which facilitates the formation of a network of hydrogen bonds within the dough.

  4. SECOND RISE:

    Punch dough down. Turn onto a lightly floured surface; divide dough in half. Shape each into a loaf. Place in 2 greased 9x5-in. loaf pans. Cover and let rise until doubled, 30-45 minutes.

    5. What's going on?


    The bread rises again. (See analysis of STEP 2 for chemical processes involved)

  5. BAKING:

    Bake at 375° for 30-35 minutes or until golden brown and bread sounds hollow when tapped. Remove from pans to wire racks to cool.

    6. The heat produces even more of a rise as the bread bakes. The increase in temperature accelerates the rate of fermentation until the dough reaches ~46°C and the yeast dies. The loaf is completely baked when it reaches an approximate temperature of 98°C. The color of the crust occurs due to the blending of sugars and other products.
